    Shelton 1
    (Pliny 1.1)

    Indefinite Pronouns/Adjectives

    This letter makes use of a new kind of pronoun/adjective: the indefinite. The indefinite pronoun/adjective indicates a generalizing sense: "someone did this," "each/every soldier fought." Specific definitions depend on the specific indefinite pronoun/adjective being used.

    The first indefinite pronoun/adjective in this letter is aliquis, aliqua, aliquid, "some(one)", "some(thing)", "any(one)", "any(thing)". A very important note about this pronoun/adjective: if it follows the words sī, nisī, num, or nē, the ali- will disappear from the front of the word. This is why the form that we see in the first sentence of the letter (...ut epistulas, si quas paulo curatius scripsissem...) is quas, rather than the expected aliquas. This can be confusing, especially since the form that's left over looks a lot like the relative pronoun. So, if you see a word that looks like a relative pronoun after si, nisi, num, or nē, odds are good that you are actually dealing with the indefinite pronoun/adjective rather than a relative pronoun.

    The best way to remember this is the following rhyme: After sī, nisī, num, and nē, all the ali-'s fall away.

    The declension chart is as follows:

    Masculine Feminine Neuter
    Sing Nom aliquis (aliquī) aliqua aliquid (aliquod)
    Gen alicuius alicuius alicuius
    Dat alicui alicui alicui
    Acc aliquem aliquam aliquid (aliquod)
    Abl aliquō aliquā aliquō
    Pl Nom aliquī aliquae aliqua
    Gen aliquōrum aliquārum aliquōrum
    Dat aliquibus aliquibus aliquibus
    Acc aliquōs aliquās aliqua
    Abl aliquibus aliquibus aliquibus

    The second indefinite in this letter takes two forms. As a pronoun, it is quisque, quidque, "each one", "every one"; as an adjective, it is quīque, quaeque, quodque, "each", "every". Note that the pronoun form is simply the interrogative pronoun (click for declension chart) with the suffix -que added to the end of any of its declined forms. The adjectival form is simply the relative pronoun (click for declension chart) with the same suffix -que added to it.

    Ablative of Degree of Difference

    With a comparative adjective or adverb, you can use an ablative adjective or noun in the neuter singular to indicate how much the degree differs between the two items being compared. Take a look at the following examples:

    • hic mīles paulō fortior illō erat.This soldier was a little stronger than that (soldier). (paulus, -a, -um, "little", "small")
    • illae feminae multō laetiorēs hīs sunt. Those women are much happier than these (women). (multus, -a, -um, "much", "many")

    Substantive Clauses:
    Noun Clause of Result

    A substantive clause or noun clause is a dependent clause, often introduced by ut, ut nōn, or nē that essentially functions like the subject or the object of a verb. We saw specific examples of this last year when we talked about certain dependent clauses. Think, for example, about indirect commands, like the following from this letter:

    • Frequenter hortatus es, ut epistulas, si quas paulo curatius scripsissem, colligerem publicaremque. You have constantly encouraged me to (literally, "that I") collect and publish my letters, if I had written any (letters) a little more carefully..

    In the preceding sentence, the indirect command ut ... colligerem publicaremque essentially functions as the object of the verb hortatus es. What did you encourage? That I collect and publish my letters. So, some of the dependent clauses that we learned about last year fall under this larger umbrella of substantive clauses.

    A new kind of substantive clause that also appears in this letter is called a noun clause of result. This dependent clause, often (but not always) introduced by ut (or ut nōn for a negative clause), uses a subjunctive verb and indicates what happens as the accomplishment of an effort. For example:
    • efficiam ut intellegās. I will make you understand. (literally, "I will bring about that you understand").

    Noun clauses of result show up often with the verb facere or its compounds, like efficere ("to bring about, effect").

    Impersonal Verbs and paenitet

    Impersonal verbs are 3rd person singular verbs that seem to take "it" as a subject: for example, "it remains (that) ..." (superest, as in section 2 of this letter) or "it is allowed (that) ..." (licet, as in Shelton 2.1). Passive forms of verbs like faciō or its compounds can also function impersonally: for example, "it was accomplished (that) ..." (effectum est). Such verbs often introduce a noun clause of result that begins with ut.

    In such sentences, however, "it" is usually a placeholder for a construction like a noun clause of result that functions as the "true" subject of the sentence. Take, for example, this sentence from section 2 of this letter:

    • ita enim fiet, ut eas ... requiram et ... non supprimam. For thus it will happen, that I will look for them and not suppress them.

    In the preceding sentence, the ut begins a noun clause of result that actually functionally serves as the subject of the sentence. While we can translate it as "it will happen that...", a more literal translation might be something like "[the fact] that I will look for them and not surpress them will happen." In these cases, the noun clause of result functions subjectivally and explains what is allowed, happening, remaining, etc.

    One special impersonal verb is paenitet, "it grieves, makes sorry, causes regret", special because of its unusual construction. It takes as objects an accusative of the person who regrets or is made sorry and a genitive of what they regret or are sorry for. A template for translating this verb, then, would be something like "[accusative] regrets/is sorry for [genitive]." (The literal translation would be something like "it grieves [accusative] of [genitive]", which is much more difficult to conceptualize.)

    Take a look at the use of this verb in section 2 of this letter:

    • superest ut nec te consilii nec me paeniteat obsequii.

    paeniteat is in the subjunctive here because it follows ut in a noun clause of result. Note that paeniteat here has two accusatives (te and me) that pair respectively with two genitives (consilii and obsequii): "It remains that you neither regret your advice nor I regret following it." (literally, "it remains that it neither grieves you of advice nor me of compliance.")

    fiō, fierī, factus sum

    There is an important irregular verb that you should become familiar with: fiō, fierī, factus sum, "to happen", "to come to pass", "to become". The third principal part may look familiar as the perfect passive of faciō -- that is because this irregular verb does often function as the passive voice of that verb, in the sense of "to be made" or "to be done".

    This verb defies easy categorizations into "passive" or "active", and while its present system forms are irregular, its perfect system forms are completely regular. In the present system, fiō largely follows the patterns of 4th conjugation verbs, except in the infinitive (fierī) and the imperfect subjunctive (fierem, fierēs, fieret...).

    Even so, the parsing of some forms can be adduced through logic. For example, the form fiet appears in section 2 of this letter. What verb tense in the 4th conjugation verb has that combination of -i- and the personal ending -et?

    All paradigms for this verb can be found in the charts at Dickinson College Commentaries' Allen and Greenough page on this verb.

    Ablative of Separation

    An ablative of separation indicates, quite literally, a separation between two things. This use of the ablative differs from the ablative of motion from in that the latter usually involves a physical place that there is movement away from. The ablative of separation can indicate a rift or separation from an abstract notion. Take, for example, this sentence in section 1 of this letter:

    • non tamen ut omnino ab inertia mea et quiete discederem. "Not, however, so that I would depart from my idleness and rest."

    inertia and quiete are abstract concepts from which Pliny would be separated by the act of going away. The ablative of separation can use a preposition like a/ab or e/ex, but in some instances, the definition of the verb is enough to necessitate an ablative of separation without the use of a preposition.

    Ablative of Means
    vs. Ablative of Agent

    We know two uses of the ablative that we can translate with the preposition "by": the ablative of means to indicate the means or instrument by which someone performs the verb's action (nautae ventīs navigāvērunt, "the sailors sailed by (means of) the winds"); and the ablative of agent with ā/ab to indicate who performed the action of a passive verb (hostēs ā mīlitibus interfectī sunt, "the enemies were killed by the soldiers.")

    You can also use an ablative of means with a passive verb to indicate agency if that agent is an inanimate object or concept rather than an actual person. Note that this means that such an ablative will not use the preposition ā/ab, which is reserved for the ablative of agent. Take a look at this example from section 2 of this letter:

    • mirum est, ut animus agitatione motuque corporis excitetur. It is remarkable how the soul is stimulated by action and motion.

    Objective and Subjective Genitive

    The uses of the genitive case that we've seen so far (possession, description) attach a genitive often to another noun to indicate who possesses that noun or what that noun consists of, respectively. In both cases, we can translate the genitive with "of".

    When the genitive is attached to a noun that indicates some kind of verbal action (e.g., "love", "care", "incitement"), the genitive might be either a subjective or objective genitive. The former indicates that the genitive would be performing that verbal action (hence, "subjective") and can be translated with "of" or like a possessive genitive, while the latter indicates that the genitive would be receiving that verbal action (hence, "objective") and can be translated with "for" or "of". Take a look at the following example:

    • amor liberorum meorum
      • subjective genitive: "love of my children" or "my children's love" (the children are the ones performing the love)
      • objective genitive: "love for my children" (the children are the ones receiving the love)

    An objective genitive occurs in section 2 of this letter:

    • silvae et solitudo ipsumque illud silentium ... magna cogitationis incitamenta sunt. "The woods and emptiness and that very silence ... are great inducements for thought."

    cogitationis, "thought", receives the action of encouraging or goading in incitamenta, so that would be an objective genitive. Note how it can't work as a subjective genitive -- "thought" cannot perform the action of "inciting" or "goading."

    Alternative 2nd Singular Passive

    When we learned about the passive voice in the present system last year, we learned that the 2nd person singular personal ending is -ris. For example, amāris, "You are loved", or amābēris, "You will be loved." (Recall that the passive personal endings are -r, -ris, -tur, -mur, -mini, -ntur)

    The 2nd person passive has an alternative ending: -re. Take a look at how it is used in section 3 of this letter:

    • proinde, cum venabere..., Therefore, when you (will) hunt...

    venābēre comes from the deponent 1st conjugation verb venor, venārī, venātus sum, "to hunt". Thus, it is passive in form but active in meaning, and since it's a 1st conjugation verb, we might expect an ending like -bēris.

    The -re and -ris endings for the 2nd singular passive can be used interchangeably, and indeed, later in that same section, Pliny uses the form experiēris.

    Be careful when you see an -re ending! The only thing that distinguishes, for example, amāre as an infinitive ('to love') and amāre as 2nd singular present passive indicative ('you are loved') is context.
